Main finding

Homes closer to high-demand schools usually sell for more. In the preferred model, the premium is 1.16% within 0-1 km and 0.41% within 1-2 km.

The boundary test (RDD) is more cautious: there is no stable, strong jump exactly at 1 km after extended checks. This means broader neighborhood demand matters, not just one cutoff line.

In simple terms

Homes nearer to high-demand schools generally sell for more. But crossing only the 1 km admission line is not, by itself, a consistently strong causal jump in price.

Therefore: policy should combine housing, school-access, and neighborhood planning instead of relying on one boundary rule alone.

Hedonic: market-wide gradient RDD: local causal test Policy: integrated planning needed

Key Insights

Findings below are summarized from the report's empirical results and robustness checks.

Preferred hedonic specification (Model 3)

After adding accessibility and school density controls, premiums are concentrated within 1 km:

0-1 km: 1.16% 1-2 km: 0.41% Adj R^2: 0.823 N: 291,419

RDD overview

Across extended specifications, the 1 km cutoff effect is not strong and stable. The evidence does not support a clear standalone causal jump at the policy boundary.

Therefore: treat school demand as part of a wider location premium, not as a single-rule effect.

Drivers of premium (who and where)

Your heterogeneity results show the premium is not uniform. It is stronger for larger family-sized flats.

Subgroup 0-1 km premium 1-2 km premium Policy reading
1-3 room flats +0.43% -2.22% Outer-band premium is weak/negative in smaller flats.
4-room and above +1.33% +1.28% Family-oriented demand shows stronger school-proximity premium.

Therefore: if MND targets affordability support, segment by flat-size groups instead of applying one uniform intervention everywhere.

Count effect

Each additional good school within 1 km is linked to roughly 0.78% higher price per sqft; within 1-2 km, only about 0.07%.

Therefore: school clustering near a unit matters most inside the 1 km zone.

Quadratic controls

With nonlinear accessibility terms, the positive school-proximity association remains: 0-1 km premium at 1.49%, 1-2 km at 0.62%.

Therefore: this result is not just a simple linear-distance artifact.

Weighting checks

GSI weighting variants produce similar premiums when SDI remains dominant, supporting stability of the main association.

Therefore: the broad finding is stable under reasonable index-weight changes.

Methodology

Two-pronged strategy from the report: hedonic regression for broad market gradients and RDD for local causal evidence at the 1 km admission cutoff.

Data and features

Final working sample includes 291,419 HDB resale transactions (2013-2025), matched with geocoded flat locations, school registration-based desirability measures, and accessibility controls (MRT, bus stop, hawker, mall, CBD).

School desirability is constructed using SDI/GSI concepts from ballot intensity, persistence, and institutional markers (GEP/SAP), with threshold checks at 75/80/85/90.

Therefore: officers can compare policy options with one consistent data backbone instead of ad-hoc case studies.

Empirical design

Hedonic model: log real price per sqft on school-proximity indicators plus structural and locational controls, estimated in sequential specifications to diagnose omitted variables.

RDD model: compares flats just inside vs outside 1 km cutoff around desirable schools, with extended versions adding school fixed effects and clustered standard errors.

Therefore: you get both a market-wide pattern view and a stricter boundary-causality check.

How this app operationalizes the methodology

The prediction tab uses trained threshold-specific artifacts and geospatially engineered features. The chat tab adds natural-language access, report-grounded retrieval (RAG), and guided slot-filling, so policy officers can obtain structured evidence without coding.

Governance safeguards are built in: no silent carry-over to final prediction, explicit confirmation of final inputs, transparent assumption reporting when `unknown` values are imputed, confidence-tier labeling, and abstain behavior when critical data is too weak.

Policy Brief

Policy interpretation from the report, structured for MND-facing communication.

Implications for MND

Proximity to desirable schools is associated with higher resale prices, which can intensify affordability pressures and spatial inequality. Demand for perceived school quality appears to be capitalized into housing values across neighborhoods.

Since the boundary-specific causal jump is not consistently strong, policy responses should not rely only on 1 km rule changes. A more effective approach is integrated housing-education planning that diffuses demand pressure across estates.

Therefore: prioritize place-based affordability measures and coordination with MOE supply planning.

Recommended positioning

Use hedonic results as evidence of broad demand-linked price gradients, and use RDD findings as a caution against over-claiming direct boundary causality.

Do: discuss equity and access planning Avoid: claiming single-rule causality

Limitations

Amenity locations are measured with current datasets rather than full historical infrastructure. Hedonic estimates may still carry omitted-variable bias from unobserved neighborhood quality. "Good school" proxies perceived demand rather than objective school quality.

Conclusion framing

Near-school premiums are visible in the broad market, but evidence for a sharp 1 km causal discontinuity is mixed. Policy strategy should therefore address wider demand and neighborhood conditions, not only boundary mechanics.
